Context.

JobNimbus had a real adoption gap in supplier ordering. Around 22% of roofing contractors were still picking up the phone to call their rep instead of placing orders through the platform.

For the business, that's revenue we never touch. For me as a designer, it was a signal worth digging into — something about the platform experience still felt riskier or harder than just calling a person.

The team's early hypothesis: people stick with reps because reps catch mistakes. A good rep knows how you work, remembers your preferences, flags a bad combo before it ships. We wanted to know if JobNimbus could fill that role.

Turns out we were half right, half off — and chasing the question was still worth it.

22% of logos prefer ordering through their supplier rep
73% of users build material orders from scratch, with no upstream validation
$85M supplier revenue target tied to ordering adoption by 2028
70 users surveyed to pressure-test the research direction

Research.

Before jumping to solutions, I needed to understand what was actually driving rep preference — not just the story we were already telling ourselves.

I interviewed owners, office managers, and production managers, and ran a broader survey across 70 users. I worked closely with Customer Success and Support to see where orders were actually breaking. One group I went after deliberately: companies that had recently lost a trusted rep — either because the rep left or their supplier got acquired. That group gave the clearest signal because the before/after contrast was immediate and sharp.

Who I talked to.

Suncraft — Garrett

Garrett had a rep who basically functioned like a human AI. He could send vague info and the rep would figure out compatible materials, catch substitutions, and flag problems before anything shipped. When that rep's company got acquired, the expertise vanished overnight. Garrett started seeing delivery errors on every other order.

Triarch — CEO

He pushed back on the whole premise — in his view, estimate time was where AI assistance would actually pay off, not material ordering. Worth noting: he was already in the 27% who convert estimates into material orders, so he had upstream validation built in. Useful perspective, but not representative of most users.

Slog Group — Juan

Juan was direct about catalog accuracy being a major pain point. He also surfaced taxes on material orders as an urgent issue affecting their QuickBooks integration. That tax theme came up more than once and wasn't on our radar early.

Legacy Roofing — Monty

Monty was doing order calculations in an external spreadsheet and had no idea JobNimbus already had that capability. He also kept a separate text thread with his supplier as a backup — a workaround that pointed to a deeper trust gap, not just a missing feature.

What the data said.

The survey's top question was basic: make sure the supplier actually receives the order. The rep preference was mostly about negotiation, relationships, and flexibility — not mistake prevention, which had been our assumption.

Interviews added the nuance surveys can't. When a trusted rep leaves, the expertise gap is real and shows up fast. And for the 73% building orders from scratch, there's basically no safety net — no estimate conversion, nothing catching errors before submission.

"Previously relied on a knowledgeable supplier rep as a 'human AI' — rep would auto-select compatible materials from basic info. QXO lacks this expertise level."

— Garrett, Suncraft Roofing

The First Idea.

Before research fully wrapped, we started exploring a concept: what if placing an order in JobNimbus felt like texting your rep? We prototyped an AI chatbot embedded in the material order form, walking users through it conversationally.

It was genuinely interesting. We built it and put it in front of customers.

What we heard

They told us it might help someone brand new. But for day-to-day work, the experienced users said they'd skip right past it. They weren't looking for a guide — they were trying to move fast through something they already knew cold.

That was a miss. But the feedback was exactly what you want: not polite rejection, but specific direction. People didn't just say no, they told us what they actually wanted instead.

The Pivot.

Across interviews, the same theme kept surfacing. Users did want AI in material ordering — just not as a replacement for the workflow or a step-by-step companion. They wanted something quiet that catches problems before those problems become expensive.

Catch a compatibility issue. Flag a missing item. Call out the wrong quantity. Basically, what a good rep does — without slowing anyone down.

The 73/27 split made the audience obvious. Most users are building from scratch with no validation at all. That's who this needed to work for.

The Design.

The core challenge wasn't how smart we could make it — it was restraint.

Experienced users move fast. If AI interrupts them, introduces a delay, or wedges itself between them and the submit button, they'll route around it. So the design needed to stay out of the way until the user chose to invoke it — and then be immediately useful.

We landed on a single Smart Check button in the Material Order header. It doesn't do anything until the user triggers it. When they do, it reviews the order and surfaces results in a slide-out panel, grouped by severity. The user stays in control the whole time.

What it reviews.

Errors — critical
  • Line items the supplier can't fulfill (out of stock)
  • Items not linked to the supplier's catalog
  • Items with no SKU at the selected supplier
Warnings — worth reviewing
  • Incomplete selections (options not yet picked)
  • Missing companion items (starter strip, underlayment, drip edge, compatible ridge vent)
  • Quantity mismatches (too few nails, or line quantities that don't match roof measurements)

In short: errors = supplier won't accept it; warnings = the order will go through, but it probably shouldn't as-is.

Every flagged item has an action attached: Find Replacement, Add Item, Remove Item, or Ignore. Batch actions let users apply or dismiss everything at once. The idea was simple: acting on a suggestion should be easier than scrolling past it.

We also added a separate Clean Up button for zero-quantity items and disconnected products. That separation was intentional — Clean Up is housekeeping, Smart Check is judgment. They shouldn't feel like the same thing.

How I prototyped and tested it.

I built Smart Check as a navigable prototype on our internal Forge server — fully interactive, hosted, easy to share async. Dropping a link in Slack and getting feedback within a few hours cut out a lot of scheduled walkthroughs that would have slowed us down.

Engineering also ran a live POC connected to a real AI backend so we could test with actual customer logos. That mattered. Real model output gave us an honest read on accuracy and trust in a way static prototypes can't. You can fake interaction patterns. You can't fake whether the AI catches the right mistakes.

Key Design Decisions.

Non-blocking users can submit with warnings unresolved

Smart Check has to earn trust through accuracy before it gets any authority. Starting as guidance gives it room to build credibility before enforcement becomes an option.

On-demand only button-triggered, never automatic

Running checks on every save would interrupt experienced users who already know what they're doing. They decide when the review happens.

Severity levels errors vs. warnings, treated differently

A missing SKU is a hard stop. A missing drip edge is a heads-up. Treating those the same trains people to ignore the whole panel.

Actionable, not informational every flag comes with a next step

A list of issues with no clear fix is just extra friction.

Future enforcement layer designed with access profiles from the start

Once accuracy is proven, managers can require Smart Check for junior estimators while leaving experienced users alone. That training angle came up repeatedly in research and shaped the permission model early.

Success Metrics.

The goal isn't universal adoption out of the gate — it's being genuinely useful when someone does run it. Until the AI proves itself, accuracy and action rate matter more than raw usage numbers.

Metric Target Why it matters
Adoption Rate 30% of orders Signals discoverability and perceived value
Action Rate 20% apply a suggestion Real usefulness vs. curiosity clicks
Error Accuracy 90% true positive rate Trust erodes fast if the AI cries wolf
Order Error Rate Decrease vs. baseline The outcome that actually matters downstream
Submission Abandonment No increase Smart Check should reduce friction, not create it

Reflections.

The chatbot prototype didn't land, but it was probably the most valuable thing we built early. When you test something that fails, and people come back with "here's what I actually wanted," you move faster than you ever do from polite approval that never converts into real behavior.

The other turning point was that 73/27 split. One data point changed who we were designing for. Without it, we likely would have kept building for the minority who already had upstream validation — the people who needed the least help. Quant gave us the confidence to make a sharp call that interviews alone couldn't justify.

I keep coming back to the non-blocking decision. From the outside, it might look like hedging — building something we weren't willing to enforce. In practice, it was the only move that made sense. You can't demand trust in an AI system before it's earned it. Let it be right enough times first. Enforcement can come later.
