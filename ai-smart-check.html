Context.

JobNimbus had a gap in supplier ordering adoption. About 22% of roofing contractors still preferred placing material orders through their supplier rep rather than through the platform.

From a business perspective: a lost transaction. From a user perspective: a signal worth paying attention to.

The team's hypothesis was that customers trusted their rep to catch mistakes. A good rep remembers your preferences, flags when something's off, stops an order from going sideways before it ships. We wanted to know if we could build that into JobNimbus.

That hypothesis turned out to be partially right, partially wrong, and worth the dig.

22% of logos prefer ordering through their supplier rep
73% of users build material orders from scratch — no upstream validation
$85M supplier revenue target tied to ordering adoption by 2028
70 users surveyed to validate the research direction

Research.

Before designing anything, I needed to understand what was actually driving rep preference — not what we assumed.

I ran customer interviews with owners, office managers, and production managers, plus a broader survey of 70 users. I worked closely with Customer Success and Support to understand where orders broke down in the field. And I specifically sought out companies that had recently lost a trusted supplier rep relationship. That group was the most illuminating.

Who I talked to.

Suncraft — Garrett

Had relied on a rep who functioned like a human AI — auto-selecting compatible materials from minimal info. When that rep's company got acquired, that expertise disappeared overnight. Now getting delivery errors every other order.

Triarch — CEO

Pushed back on material orders as the right place for AI validation — felt estimate time was more valuable. But this customer was in the 27% who convert estimates to material orders. Their view didn't represent the majority.

Slog Group — Juan

Flagged catalog accuracy as a top pain point. Also surfaced taxes on material orders as a high-urgency gap affecting QuickBooks integration.

Legacy Roofing — Monty

Using an external spreadsheet for calculations without knowing JobNimbus had the same feature built in. Had set up a parallel text chat with the supplier as a fail-safe — a reliability workaround that pointed at a deeper trust gap.

What the data told us.

The survey's #1 ask: guarantee the supplier receives the order. Rep preference was driven by negotiation, flexibility, and relationship — not mistake prevention.

But the interviews told us something more specific. When a trusted rep leaves, the expertise gap is genuinely painful. And for anyone building an order without an estimate — the 73% — there's no safety net at all.

"Previously relied on a knowledgeable supplier rep as a 'human AI' — rep would auto-select compatible materials from basic info. QXO lacks this expertise level."

— Garrett, Suncraft Roofing

The First Idea.

Before research wrapped, the team had already started exploring a concept: what if ordering through JobNimbus felt like texting your supplier rep? An AI chatbot embedded in the material order form, guiding users conversationally.

It was a compelling idea. We built and tested a prototype.

What we heard

Customers could see it helping someone brand new to ordering. For their actual day-to-day users, it would go untouched. Experienced users wanted speed, not guidance.

We missed the mark — but the way customers told us that was just as valuable as the miss, because they didn't just say no. They told us what they actually wanted.

The Pivot.

Across multiple interviews, the same themes kept surfacing. Customers wanted AI in material ordering — just applied differently. Not something that replaces the process. Something that quietly protects it.

They wanted AI to catch human error before submission. Compatibility issues, missing items, wrong quantities — the specific things a good rep would catch.

The 73/27 data point sealed the direction. Only 27% of users convert from an estimate and already have upstream validation. The other 73% build from scratch with nothing.

The Design.

The central challenge was restraint.

Experienced users want to move fast. Any AI that interrupted their flow, added latency, or blocked submission would get dismissed — or worked around. The design had to earn its place.

The result: a single Smart Check button in the Material Order header that does nothing until the user asks it to. When triggered, it reviews the full order and surfaces results in a slide-out panel, organized by severity. The user stays in control at every step.

What it reviews.

Errors — Critical
  • SKU not found, product unavailable at branch, catalog match failure
Warnings — Worth Reviewing
  • Missing starter strip, missing underlayment, missing ridge cap or drip edge, potentially incompatible combinations

Every flagged item has a direct action: Find Replacement, Add Item, Remove Item, or Ignore. Batch actions let users apply or dismiss everything at once. Acting on a suggestion should be faster than scrolling past it.

A separate Clean Up button handles zero-quantity items and disconnected products. Keeping these distinct was deliberate — Clean Up is a utility. Smart Check is intelligence. They should feel different.

How I prototyped and tested it.

I built Smart Check as a navigable prototype on the internal Forge server — fully interactive, hosted, accessible asynchronously. Dropping a URL in Slack and getting anchored feedback within hours cut out a lot of scheduled walkthrough time.

The team also ran a live engineering POC wired to a real AI backend for logo testing with actual customers. Genuine AI responses — not simulated ones — gave us much more honest signal on accuracy and user trust than a static prototype ever could.

Key Design Decisions.

Non-blocking User can submit with warnings unresolved

Users can submit with warnings unresolved. Smart Check needs to prove its accuracy before it earns enforcement authority. Starting as a suggestion, not a requirement, lets us build trust before asking more.

On-demand only Button-triggered, never automatic

Auto-running on every save would interrupt experienced users who already know what they're doing. Users control when the review happens.

Severity levels Errors vs. warnings

Not all flags are equal. A missing SKU is a hard blocker. A missing drip edge is a heads-up. Treating both the same would erode trust — users would start ignoring everything. Separating errors from warnings lets them triage appropriately.

Actionable, not informational Every flag has a next step

A list of problems with no way to fix them creates frustration — every item has a direct action so users can resolve without leaving the panel.

Future enforcement layer Behind access profiles

Once accuracy is proven, managers will be able to require Smart Check for junior estimators while leaving experienced users alone. Training new estimators was the highest-value use case from research, and it shaped the permission model from day one.

Roadmap.

Smart Check is shipping incrementally — each phase validating accuracy and adoption before the next layer is added.

Complete Phase 1 — POC & Logo Testing

Hosted proof-of-concept wired to a real AI backend — tested with live customer logos for genuine accuracy signal.

Complete Phase 2 — Agent Foundation

Core AI agent infrastructure — knowledge base, manufacturer specs, compatibility rules. The foundation everything else builds on.

In Progress Phase 3 — Image to Material Order

Upload a photo of a paper order or document and auto-generate a material order — meeting roofers where they actually work.

Upcoming Phase 4 — Full Smart Check Engine

Complete error and warning detection: SKU validation, compatibility checking, missing item identification.

Upcoming Phase 5 — Suggested Products

Proactively surface the next logical product — moving Smart Check from reactive to anticipatory.

Planned Phase 6–7 — AI Add-Ons & Sales Workflow

Post-MVP expansion into the sales workflow and supplier-side intelligence.

Success Metrics.

Smart Check's first job isn't to be used by everyone — it's to be genuinely useful when it is used. Accuracy and action rate matter more than raw adoption until the AI has earned users' trust.

Metric Target Why it matters
Adoption Rate 30% of orders Signals discoverability and perceived value
Action Rate 20% apply a suggestion Real usefulness vs. curiosity clicks
Error Accuracy 90% true positive rate Trust erodes fast if the AI cries wolf
Order Error Rate Decrease vs. baseline The outcome that actually matters downstream
Submission Abandonment No increase Smart Check should catch friction, not create it

Reflections.

A miss is only a miss if you don't listen

The chatbot prototype didn't land — but the feedback it generated was the most useful thing we produced in the whole project. Testing early and listening closely to what customers actually said pointed us directly at the right problem.

One data point can reframe everything

The 73/27 split changed who we were designing for. Without it, we would have kept optimizing for the wrong audience. Quantitative research gave us permission to make a sharp call that qualitative research alone couldn't have justified.

AI works best as a guardrail, not a guide

The chatbot tried to guide users through a process they already knew. Smart Check waits quietly and only speaks up when something's wrong. The difference in reception was immediate — because one fit how people work, and one asked them to work differently.

Trust is earned in increments

Making Smart Check non-blocking wasn't hedging — it was product strategy. You can't ask users to trust an AI on day one. You have to prove it's right more often than it's wrong before you give it any authority over their workflow.
