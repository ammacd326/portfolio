Context.

JobNimbus had a gap in supplier ordering adoption. About 22% of roofing contractors still preferred placing material orders through their supplier rep rather than through the platform.

From a business perspective: a lost transaction. From a user perspective: worth figuring out why.

The team's working hypothesis was that customers trusted their rep to catch mistakes. A good rep remembers your preferences, flags when something looks off, stops an order from going sideways before it ships. We wanted to know if we could build that into JobNimbus.

That hypothesis turned out to be partially right, partially wrong, and worth the dig.

22% of logos prefer ordering through their supplier rep
73% of users build material orders from scratch — no upstream validation
$85M supplier revenue target tied to ordering adoption by 2028
70 users surveyed to validate the research direction

Research.

Before designing anything, I needed to understand what was actually driving rep preference — not what we assumed was driving it.

I ran interviews with owners, office managers, and production managers, plus a broader survey of 70 users. I worked closely with Customer Success and Support to understand where orders broke down in the field. And I specifically sought out companies that had recently lost a trusted supplier rep — either the rep left, or their company got acquired. That group ended up being the most useful.

Who I talked to.

Suncraft — Garrett

Had relied on a rep who basically functioned as a human AI — give him minimal info and he'd auto-select compatible materials, catch substitutions, flag issues before they shipped. When that rep's company got acquired, that expertise just vanished. Garrett was now getting delivery errors every other order.

Triarch — CEO

Pushed back on material orders as the right place for AI — felt estimate time was where it would be more valuable. Worth noting though: this customer was in the 27% who convert estimates to material orders. He already had upstream validation. His experience wasn't representative of most users.

Slog Group — Juan

Flagged catalog accuracy as a major pain point. Also surfaced taxes on material orders as a high-urgency gap affecting their QuickBooks integration — that one came up a few times and wasn't on our radar going in.

Legacy Roofing — Monty

Was using an external spreadsheet for calculations, didn't know JobNimbus had that built in. Had also set up a parallel text thread with his supplier as a backup — a workaround that pointed at something deeper than a feature gap.

What the data told us.

The survey's top ask was straightforward: guarantee the supplier actually receives the order. Rep preference was about negotiation, flexibility, and relationship — not mistake prevention, which was what we'd assumed.

But the interviews added texture. When a trusted rep disappears, the expertise gap is real and immediate. And for the 73% of users building orders from scratch without an estimate, there's no safety net at all — no upstream validation, nothing catching errors before submission.

"Previously relied on a knowledgeable supplier rep as a 'human AI' — rep would auto-select compatible materials from basic info. QXO lacks this expertise level."

— Garrett, Suncraft Roofing

The First Idea.

Before research wrapped, the team had started exploring a direction: what if ordering through JobNimbus felt like texting your supplier rep? An AI chatbot embedded in the material order form, guiding users through it conversationally.

It was a genuinely compelling idea. We built and tested a prototype.

What we heard

Customers could see it helping someone brand new to ordering. For their actual day-to-day users — the experienced ones — it would go untouched. They wanted to move fast, not be guided through something they already knew how to do.

We missed. But the way customers told us that was just as useful as the miss itself — they didn't just say no. They told us what they actually wanted instead.

The Pivot.

The same themes kept coming up across interviews. Customers wanted AI in material ordering — just not like that. Not something that replaced the process or walked them through it. Something that ran quietly in the background and caught problems before they became someone else's problem.

Catch the compatibility issue. Flag the missing item. Surface the wrong quantity. The specific things a good rep would have caught.

The 73/27 split made the direction clear. Most users are building orders from scratch with nothing checking their work. That's who we were designing for.

The Design.

The central challenge was restraint.

Experienced users move fast. Any AI that interrupted their flow, added latency, or got between them and the submit button would get ignored — or worked around. The design had to earn its place by staying out of the way until it was actually needed.

The result: a single Smart Check button in the Material Order header that does nothing until the user triggers it. When they do, it reviews the full order and surfaces results in a slide-out panel, organized by severity. The user stays in control throughout.

What it reviews.

Errors — Critical
  • SKU not found, product unavailable at branch, catalog match failure
Warnings — Worth Reviewing
  • Missing starter strip, missing underlayment, missing ridge cap or drip edge, potentially incompatible combinations

Every flagged item has a direct action: Find Replacement, Add Item, Remove Item, or Ignore. Batch actions let users apply or dismiss everything at once. The bar for acting on a suggestion should be lower than scrolling past it.

There's also a separate Clean Up button for zero-quantity items and disconnected products. Keeping those distinct was intentional — Clean Up is maintenance. Smart Check is judgment. They should feel like different things.

How I prototyped and tested it.

I built Smart Check as a navigable prototype on the internal Forge server — fully interactive, hosted, shareable asynchronously. Dropping a URL in Slack and getting real feedback within a few hours cut out a lot of scheduled walkthrough time that would have slowed us down.

The engineering team also ran a live POC wired to a real AI backend for testing with actual customer logos. That mattered — genuine AI responses gave us honest signal on accuracy and trust in a way a static prototype couldn't have. You can fake a lot of things in a prototype. You can't fake whether the AI actually catches the right errors.

Key Design Decisions.

Non-blocking users can submit with warnings unresolved

Smart Check needs to prove its accuracy before it earns any enforcement authority. Starting as a suggestion lets it build trust before asking for more.

On-demand only button-triggered, never automatic

Auto-running on every save would interrupt experienced users who already know what they're doing. They decide when the review happens.

Severity levels errors vs. warnings

A missing SKU is a blocker. A missing drip edge is a heads-up. Treating them the same would train users to ignore everything. Separating them lets people triage.

Actionable, not just informational every flag has a next step

A list of problems with no clear path to fixing them is just friction. Every item resolves without leaving the panel.

Future enforcement layer behind access profiles

Once accuracy is proven, managers can require Smart Check for junior estimators while leaving experienced users alone. That use case — training new estimators — came up repeatedly in research and shaped the permission model from the start.

Roadmap.

Smart Check is shipping incrementally — each phase validating accuracy and adoption before the next layer is added.

Complete Phase 1 — POC & Logo Testing

Hosted proof-of-concept wired to a real AI backend, tested with live customer logos.

Complete Phase 2 — Agent Foundation

Core AI infrastructure: knowledge base, manufacturer specs, compatibility rules.

In Progress Phase 3 — Image to Material Order

Upload a photo of a paper order and auto-generate a material order. Meeting roofers where they actually work.

Upcoming Phase 4 — Full Smart Check Engine

Complete error and warning detection: SKU validation, compatibility checking, missing item identification.

Upcoming Phase 5 — Suggested Products

Proactively surface the next logical product, moving Smart Check from reactive to anticipatory.

Planned Phase 6–7 — AI Add-Ons & Sales Workflow

Post-MVP expansion into the sales workflow and supplier-side intelligence.

Success Metrics.

Smart Check's first job isn't to be used by everyone — it's to be genuinely useful when it is used. Until the AI has proven itself, accuracy and action rate matter more than raw adoption.

Metric Target Why it matters
Adoption Rate 30% of orders Signals discoverability and perceived value
Action Rate 20% apply a suggestion Real usefulness vs. curiosity clicks
Error Accuracy 90% true positive rate Trust erodes fast if the AI cries wolf
Order Error Rate Decrease vs. baseline The outcome that actually matters downstream
Submission Abandonment No increase Smart Check should reduce friction, not create it

Reflections.

The chatbot prototype was a miss, but it was probably the most useful thing we made. When you test something that doesn't land and the feedback is specific — not just "this isn't for me" but "here's what I actually want" — that's a better outcome than testing something that gets polite approval and then never gets used. We found the right problem because we got the first one wrong in front of real customers.

The 73/27 split was the other turning point. That single data point changed who we were designing for. Without it, we'd have kept building for the minority of users who already had validation upstream — the ones who needed it least. Quant research gave us permission to make a sharp call that interviews alone couldn't have.

The thing I keep coming back to is the non-blocking decision. It probably looked like hedging from the outside — why build something you're not willing to enforce? But it was actually the only defensible product strategy. You can't ask users to trust an AI that hasn't proven itself yet. You have to let it be right enough times first. The enforcement layer is coming. It just needs to earn it.
