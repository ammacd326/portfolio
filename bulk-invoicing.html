The starting point

This project came out of a broader UX refresh of Filevine's application — a push to modernize the product and close gaps with competitors. I was the designated designer for everything billing-related. One of the specific asks from leadership was batch invoicing: competitors had it, we didn't, and the assumption was that we needed to build it.

My PM and I could have just executed on that. Instead, we decided to validate it first. That instinct ended up being the most important decision on the project.

The first direction

I started by doing competitive research — primarily looking at QuickBooks and a few other accounting tools to understand how batch invoicing typically worked and how it might fit into Filevine's existing billing workflows. From there I built out a full high-fidelity prototype for batch invoice creation from unbilled items.

Going straight to high-fidelity was a deliberate call — we had validation testing planned, so the goal was to put something concrete in front of customers as quickly as possible. In retrospect, earlier lo-fi testing might have surfaced what we found sooner and saved some design time. That's something I've carried into how I approach validation since.

What testing told us

Our UX researcher, my PM, and I ran validation sessions with five current customers, walking them through the batch billing prototype. We asked how they'd expect certain interactions to work, what resonated, what didn't, and whether this actually solved the problems they were running into in the product today.

The feedback was consistent across all five sessions: customers didn't need to group invoices into batches. What they actually needed was the ability to create multiple invoices at once — without having to go project by project, one at a time, the way the product currently worked.

The real problem: Admins managing invoices needed to act on everything unbilled in one go. The current product forced them to do it project by project. Batch billing — grouping invoices together — didn't solve that. Bulk creation did.

It was a meaningful distinction, and one we wouldn't have caught without testing. The feature leadership had asked for wasn't wrong in spirit — it was trying to solve a real pain point — it was just solving it in the wrong way.

Changing direction

My PM and I took what we learned and made the call to pivot. Rather than scrapping the work entirely, we scaled back the batch billing design to become bulk invoicing — the ability to create invoices for all unbilled items at once, without any grouping or batching logic. Because most of the foundational design work was already done, the pivot wasn't a heavy lift.

We had hoped to run another round of validation on the updated direction, but struggled to recruit participants — unmoderated testing through Maze didn't get traction either. It wasn't ideal, but the signal from the first round of testing was clear enough that we felt confident moving forward.

We presented the solution at Filevine's betting table — a stakeholder review with the head of product and key decision makers — and it was accepted for development. Given the six-week development cycle, we scoped it into two phases: the unbilled tab first, then the invoices tab in a follow-on cycle.

What I took away

The thing I keep coming back to with this project is how easy it would have been to just build what we were asked to build. Batch invoicing was on the roadmap, competitors had it, and there was a clear brief. Pushing to validate first wasn't the path of least resistance.

But that validation session is what made the difference between shipping something customers would have ignored and shipping something that actually solved their problem. Good process doesn't always mean more time — sometimes it just means asking the right question before you start building.
