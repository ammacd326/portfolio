Filevine · Product Design

A2P 10DLC Registration

Company Filevine
My Role Product Designer
Domain LegalTech · Compliance
Type New Feature · Compliance
Timeline 5 Weeks
TL;DR

New federal SMS regulations meant Filevine customers needed to register their businesses and phone numbers before a hard deadline — or face service disruptions. I designed the full registration flow in five weeks, presented to C-level executives, shipped on time, and introduced a notification pattern that made it into the design system.

The setup.

In 2021, wireless carriers rolled out new rules for application-to-person messaging — the A2P 10DLC regulations. The short version: any company sending texts through a platform like Filevine needed to register their use case and phone numbers with the carriers, or messages would start failing or getting flagged as spam.

For law firms, that's not a minor inconvenience. A lot of them run client communication almost entirely through text. If numbers went dark mid-case, the downstream impact could be significant. The carriers set the deadline, not us. We had five weeks.

It was an odd project for my team. We normally handle billing and time tracking. This landed with us because we had capacity — which meant diving into a compliance requirement we'd never touched before, fast, with no ramp-up time. I won't pretend that didn't feel like a lot at first.

The scope turned out to be bigger than it looked. Every org in Filevine had to register independently. Every phone number needed its own enrollment. There were two tiers — one free with daily sending limits, one paid — and the experience had to handle both without burying the majority of users who only needed the free path in options that didn't apply to them.

The framing that shaped everything: nobody wanted to do this. It wasn't a feature anyone asked for. It was a compliance requirement dropped on legal teams with a hard deadline. My job as the designer was to make it as painless as possible — get in, get registered, get out.

How I approached it.

There was no existing pattern to work from, so I mapped the full user flow from scratch. The goal for the primary path — Tier 1, the free option — was to keep it clean enough that most users never had to think about the paid tier at all.

The existing number management area combined text and fax in one place, which wasn't going to work here. I split them into separate sections and added a dedicated registration area. A small structural shift, but it let us surface status clearly without disrupting anything else in the app.

The timeline forced a communication cadence I don't usually run — direct check-ins with the CEO and technical director from early on. Not standard for our team. But it helped. Decisions that might normally take weeks got made in days. Alignment happened faster because the right people were in the loop from the start.

Most of those five weeks went into detailed mocks, testing, and writing the copy for the experience. The copy mattered as much as anything else. Registration flows live or die on whether people understand each prompt. Get that wrong and the whole thing falls apart.

A component I didn't see coming.

The registration flow itself wasn't the hardest design problem. The harder part was showing status across four states — complete, pending, failed, and none — in a way that was clear, consistent, and felt like it belonged in the app rather than bolted on.

Our design system didn't have a status banner that could handle that range. I went through more iterations on that one component than anything else in the project. Eventually, with input from a senior designer, we landed on something solid.

What happened next was unexpected: other teams picked it up and it got pulled into the broader design system. Something that started as a fix for one compliance flow became a pattern the whole app now uses.

The rollout.

We shipped in five weeks. Hundreds of Filevine users registered before the carrier deadline. No service interruptions. For firms that depend on texting for client communication, continuity was everything — and they probably never thought about what kept things running smoothly. That's fine. That's part of the job.

5 weeks from kickoff to launch
100s of legal firms enrolled before the deadline
0 outages
1 new design component, now used across the full product

What I took away.

Some design work stays invisible — the problem isn't flashy, the timeline is tight, and success just means nothing breaks. I think real skill lives there too. Making a compliance process feel simple takes more effort than it looks like from the outside, and it doesn't come with a lot of fanfare.

This project also showed me that constraints don't have to be the enemy of good work. You move faster. You make harder calls earlier. That executive-level visibility, which sounds stressful in theory, actually sharpened the work — every decision needed to be defensible, and that turned out to be a pretty good way to operate.
