A2P 10DLC Registration

Company Filevine
My Role Product Designer
Domain LegalTech · Compliance

Timeline 5 Weeks
TL;DR

New federal SMS regulations meant Filevine customers needed to register their businesses and phone numbers before a hard deadline — or face service disruptions. I designed the full registration flow in five weeks, presented to C-level executives, shipped on time, and introduced a notification pattern that made it into the design system.

The situation

In 2021, wireless carriers introduced new regulations around application-to-person SMS messaging — known as A2P 10DLC. The short version: any business sending text messages through a platform like Filevine now needed to register their use case and phone numbers with carriers, or risk their messages failing to deliver or being flagged as spam.

For law firms using Filevine to communicate with clients, that wasn't a minor inconvenience. SMS is a core part of how many firms operate. Unregistered numbers going dark would have been a real problem for hundreds of customers.

The deadline was set by the carriers — not us. We had five weeks.

An unusual project for our team

My team was normally focused on billing and timekeeping. We picked this up because we were the only team with bandwidth at the time — which meant stepping into unfamiliar territory quickly and getting up to speed on a technically complex regulatory requirement without much runway.

The scope was significant. Every org within the Filevine platform needed to register their business, and every phone number used for texting needed to be registered individually. There were two registration tiers — one free with a daily message cap, one paid with higher limits — and the experience needed to handle both clearly without overwhelming users who would mostly just need the free tier.

The constraint that shaped everything: This wasn't a feature customers were asking for — it was something they had to do. The design job was to make a mandatory compliance process feel as simple and low-friction as possible, so firms could get registered and get back to work.

How I approached it

Because this was a brand new interaction with no existing pattern to reference, I started by mapping out the full user flow. Most users would only need Tier 1 registration, so I wanted to make sure the default path was clean and straightforward, with the paid tier available but not in the way.

The existing phone number management section had texting and fax numbers combined in one view — not ideal for what we needed. I restructured it into two separate tabs and added a dedicated registration tab, which gave us a clear place to surface registration status and guide users through the process without disrupting the existing functionality.

Given the timeline and the stakes, I moved through multiple rounds of design reviews quickly — including executive reviews directly with the CEO and Sr. Director of Technology. That level of visibility wasn't typical for our team, but this project warranted it. Those reviews were genuinely useful — they kept us aligned on the right problems and gave us a fast feedback loop that compressed what would normally take much longer.

The bulk of the five weeks went into high-fidelity designs, prototyping, and refining the UX copy. Getting the language right mattered a lot here — registration flows live or die on whether users understand what's being asked of them at each step.

A design system contribution I didn't expect

One of the trickier design problems on this project wasn't the registration flow itself — it was figuring out how to communicate registration status clearly across different states. Users needed to know whether their org was registered, pending, failed, or unregistered, and that information needed to be visible and consistent throughout the section.

We didn't have a notification banner in our design system that could handle multiple states like this. I went through a lot of iterations trying to get it right — something that felt native to the product rather than bolted on. With input from a senior designer on the team, we landed on a pattern that worked.

What I didn't expect was that the banner ended up being adopted into the broader design system as a new pattern for other teams to use. A component that started as a problem I needed to solve for one project ended up contributing something lasting to the product.

How it landed

We shipped within the five-week window. Hundreds of Filevine customers were able to register their organizations and phone numbers before the carrier regulations took effect — with no service disruptions. For firms that relied on SMS to communicate with clients, that continuity was the whole point.

For a project that came to our team unexpectedly, required working outside our usual domain, and carried a hard external deadline, delivering a complete, polished solution on time felt like a real win — for the team and for our customers.

What I took away

This project is a good example of a type of design work that doesn't always get talked about — the kind where the problem isn't interesting, the timeline is brutal, and the success condition is just that nothing breaks for your customers. There's real craft in making that kind of work feel effortless to the people using it.

It also reinforced something I believe pretty strongly: tight timelines and executive scrutiny aren't obstacles to good design — they're just different constraints to work within. You move faster, you prioritize harder, and you make sure every decision you make is one you can defend.
