Filevine · Product Design

A2P 10DLC Registration

Company Filevine
My Role Product Designer
Domain LegalTech · Compliance
Type New Feature · Compliance
Timeline 5 Weeks
TL;DR

New federal SMS regulations meant Filevine customers needed to register their businesses and phone numbers before a hard deadline — or face service disruptions. I designed the full registration flow in five weeks, presented to C-level executives, shipped on time, and introduced a notification pattern that made it into the design system.

The situation.

In 2021, wireless carriers rolled out new regulations for application-to-person SMS — A2P 10DLC. The short version: any business sending texts through a platform like Filevine had to register their use case and phone numbers with carriers, or their messages would start failing or getting flagged as spam.

For law firms, that's not a minor thing. A lot of firms run client communication almost entirely through SMS. Numbers going dark mid-case would have been a real problem for hundreds of customers.

The deadline was set by the carriers. Not us. We had five weeks.

An unusual project for our team.

My team owned billing and timekeeping. This came to us because we were the only team with open bandwidth — which meant getting up to speed on a technically complex compliance requirement we'd never touched, fast, with no real runway.

I won't pretend that wasn't a little stressful.

The scope was bigger than it initially looked. Every org in Filevine needed to register their business. Every individual phone number used for texting needed its own registration. There were two tiers — one free with a daily cap, one paid — and the experience had to handle both without making the majority of users, who just needed the free tier, wade through complexity that didn't apply to them.

The constraint that shaped everything.

Nobody wanted to do this. It wasn't a feature request — it was a compliance requirement dropped on law firms with a hard deadline. The design job was to make it feel as painless as possible. Get registered, get out, get back to work.

How I approached it.

There was no existing pattern to reference, so I started by mapping the full user flow from scratch. I wanted the default path — Tier 1, free registration — to be clean enough that most users never had to think about the paid option.

The existing phone number management section had texting and fax numbers in the same view, which wasn't going to work for what we needed. I split them into separate tabs and added a dedicated registration tab — a small restructure that gave us a clean place to surface registration status without breaking what was already there.

The timeline pushed me into a review cadence I wouldn't normally have — including direct reviews with the CEO and Sr. Director of Technology. That's not typical for my team. It was useful, though. Faster feedback loops, quicker alignment, decisions that would have taken weeks happening in days.

Most of the five weeks went into high-fidelity designs, prototyping, and UX copy. The copy especially — registration flows depend entirely on users understanding what they're being asked to do at each step. Get that wrong and the whole thing falls apart.

A design system contribution I didn't see coming.

The registration flow itself wasn't the hardest design problem. The hardest part was figuring out how to show registration status across four different states — registered, pending, failed, unregistered — in a way that was visible, consistent, and felt like it belonged in the product rather than being stapled onto it.

We didn't have a notification banner in the design system that could handle that kind of multi-state logic. I went through more iterations on that component than anything else on the project. Eventually, with input from a senior designer, we landed on something that worked.

What I didn't expect: it got picked up by other teams and pulled into the broader design system. A component I built to solve one specific problem ended up becoming a pattern the whole product uses.

How it landed.

We shipped inside the five-week window. Hundreds of Filevine customers registered before the regulations hit. No service disruptions.

For firms relying on SMS to talk to clients, continuity was the whole point. They probably never thought about what it took to make sure nothing changed for them. That's fine. That's the job.

5 weeks from brief to shipped
100s of law firms registered before the deadline
0 service disruptions
1 new design system component, now platform-wide

What I took away.

There's a category of design work nobody really talks about — where the problem is unglamorous, the timeline is brutal, and winning just means nothing breaks. I think there's real craft in that. Making a compliance flow feel simple is harder than it looks, and it doesn't come with a lot of recognition.

This project also reminded me that pressure doesn't have to be the enemy of good work. You move faster. You make harder calls sooner. Executive visibility that might feel intimidating in another context actually made the work sharper — every decision had to be defensible, and that's not a bad way to work.
