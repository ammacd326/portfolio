The problem.

Law firms bleed money on expenses — not because people don't care, but because the whole process is broken.

Attorneys manually comb through card statements, chase down receipts, enter the same charge in multiple places, then try to remember which client matter it belonged to. One attorney told us she was spending close to 100 hours a year just keeping expenses straight.

The risk isn't theoretical either. If a reimbursable charge doesn't get logged, the firm eats it. In a smaller practice, that kind of leak can swing real outcomes.

What stood out during research was how little was purpose-built for the way law firms actually run. Plenty of general spend tools exist, but nothing that understood the tie between an expense and a matter — and nothing that fit naturally into the tools firms rely on every day. That gap became AffiniPay's top strategic focus for over two years. I was brought in as the only designer to build the product from scratch.

Getting research right before touching design.

My PM ran initial discovery — 25+ customer interviews across attorneys, paralegals, office managers, and firm owners. My job was to make sure that learning didn't die in a slide deck. It had to show up in the product.

What I walked away with wasn't a tidy list of pain points. It was a clear picture of who was carrying the load, how much time it cost, and how much stress it created.

"I live in abject terror of you forgetting to put this in the database, because if you do, I will not get paid back. And if I gave away $700 to make $550, we're all going to be unemployed."

— Firm Owner, Real Estate Law

That emotional weight changed how we framed the MVP. We weren't just trying to speed things up — we were trying to give people a sense that they had a grip on their firm's money. That difference kept showing up in later design decisions.

In parallel, I did a competitive review of spend tools outside of legal — Ramp, Divvy, Stripe, Float. I looked closely at onboarding, card management, real-time alerts, receipt capture, and categorization. The goal wasn't to copy patterns. It was to understand what modern financial tooling feels like, then figure out where legal needed something different.

Designing for multiple roles, on multiple platforms.

Smart Spend was never a single-user product. A paralegal grabbing a receipt on their phone doesn't behave like an office manager running firm-wide spend on a desktop dashboard. An accountant reconciling at month-end is a third world entirely.

Early on I mapped separate journeys for each role — cardholder mobile flows, admin desktop management, expense-to-matter linking, reporting, accounting sync. Each path needed to work on its own, and the whole thing still had to feel like one product. That tension was constant.

The simplest insight ended up driving the mobile experience: if capture doesn't happen in the moment, it often doesn't happen at all. Attorneys aren't going to reconstruct a week of purchases on a Friday afternoon.

So we built an SMS-triggered notification that prompts a cardholder to link a transaction right after the charge clears — while the context is still fresh. That flow became one of the defining behaviors of the product.

"I always forget to link stuff. Having something that pops up and basically puts it in front of my face is friggin awesome."

— Solo Practitioner, Administrative and Criminal Law

Testing and iterating, with real pressure.

I owned usability testing end to end — study planning, recruiting, moderated sessions, synthesis, and translating findings into design changes. We ran two formal rounds, then kept learning through alpha and beta.

Round one was admin-focused: 23 sessions on the desktop dashboard, transaction management, card issuance, and permissions.

Round two shifted to the cardholder experience on mobile: 11 sessions on the core transaction flow.

What made it higher stakes than usual was who was testing. Our CEO and CFO were part of alpha. Decisions had to be defensible with evidence, not just taste.

Feedback was positive but specific. People wanted more context on the expense-linking screen, more detail in transaction tables, and a clearer distinction between raw bank data and the fields they were adding themselves. Small adjustments on paper — but they changed confidence levels immediately.

72 Combined SUS score across alpha and beta, above enterprise tooling norms
22+ Average transactions per activated card in month one across the first 10 beta firms
200+ Firms on the beta waitlist before general availability
34 Total usability sessions across two formal rounds

Twenty-two transactions per activated card in the first month isn't a novelty signup. It's a product getting absorbed into daily work.

The constraints we worked inside.

A design system being built while we shipped.

AffiniPay was mid-rebrand, pulling products into a new system that was still under construction. I ended up as the test case — designing with unfinished components and occasionally getting ahead of the system entirely.

External banking and card partners.

Smart Spend depended on deep integrations with banking and card-issuing partners. Syncing timelines was hard and affected the release schedule in ways we couldn't always control.

One designer for everything.

For the full 2.5 years, I was the only designer and researcher on the product. Strategy, UX, UI, and final polish all ran through me. That forced ruthless prioritization — and clarity about when to move fast and when to slow down and be exact.

Where it landed.

By the time I transitioned off, Smart Spend had cleared alpha and beta with strong engagement, a 200+ firm waitlist, and a 72 SUS score that backed up what we were hearing in interviews — it wasn't just functional, people could use it without friction.

What I took away.

If someone asked what I can do, this is the project I'd point to. Not because it was flawless — it wasn't — but because it demanded work across every layer of product design at once, with real stakes and not much room to hide.

Owning research, testing, systems thinking, stakeholder communication, and execution simultaneously changes how you make decisions. You can't hand problems off and hope for the best. You carry the whole thing, sort what matters from what doesn't, and keep pushing.

What I'm most proud of isn't any single screen. It's that we built something genuinely new for an industry that's been overlooked by design for a long time — and firms were lining up to use it before it even shipped.
