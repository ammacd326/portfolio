The context

Bridge was a custom platform built for Utah Foster Care — a place where UFC employees and foster parents could communicate, collaborate, and build a supportive community around foster children. The mission behind it was genuinely meaningful. The adoption problem was getting in the way of it.

UFC employees were logging in, getting confused, and going back to the tools they already knew. The feedback was consistent: people didn't understand the purpose of different sections or how to navigate the platform. And with foster parents about to be onboarded, the team didn't want to introduce a new group of users to a product that existing users were already struggling with.

We needed a solution, and we had three weeks.

Starting with research

Before jumping into designs, I wanted to understand what actually makes a product tour work — because a bad onboarding experience can be worse than no onboarding at all. I looked at a wide range of products and UI patterns, focusing on what drove comprehension and retention versus what felt like noise users would click through without reading.

That research shaped how I thought about what we were building. The goal wasn't to show users everything — it was to orient them quickly around the things that would make them feel confident enough to keep going.

The design challenge: Bridge's navigation was fully customizable — section names and icons could be changed per program. The tour had to work dynamically, pulling in whatever names UFC had chosen rather than relying on hardcoded labels. That added a layer of technical complexity that shaped what was feasible.

Deciding what to highlight

I worked with our product owner and UFC's customer success manager to decide which features the tour actually needed to cover. We kept the scope tight — the main navigation, how to start a chat, the key features within chat including video, and how to reach the support team.

That last one mattered more than it might seem. If users got stuck and didn't know how to get help, they'd disengage entirely. Making support visible and accessible was as important as explaining the features themselves.

Working within real constraints

With three weeks on the clock, I brought research-informed sketches to the team early — before committing to high-fidelity work — specifically to pressure-test what was actually buildable. That conversation with the developer shaped the final design more than anything else. There were things I wanted to do that weren't feasible in the time we had, and iterating on sketches made those tradeoffs cheaper to navigate.

We went through a few rounds of iteration before landing on a final direction that worked within both the technical constraints and the timeline. From there I moved into high-fidelity designs for the finished tour.

How it landed

We shipped before the foster parent onboarding began. The tour gave both new and existing users a structured way to understand the platform from the moment they logged in — addressing the exact confusion UFC had been flagging.

The fact that it's still live today says something. A lot of quick-turnaround solutions get replaced or removed when something better comes along. This one stuck.

3 Weeks from brief to shipped
2 User groups successfully onboarded — UFC employees and foster parents
Still Live today — the tour remains in production
1st Professional project — delivered on time with real users and real stakes

What I took away

This was an early project for me, and looking back, what stands out is how much the constraints forced good decisions. A three-week timeline with a hard external deadline meant I couldn't afford to over-design. Bringing sketches to the developer early instead of waiting for polished mockups saved time and surfaced problems before they became expensive. Those habits have stayed with me.

The stakes also weren't abstract. The people using this platform were foster care workers and families trying to support kids in the system. Designing something that actually helped them use a tool they needed felt like a good reason to get it right.
