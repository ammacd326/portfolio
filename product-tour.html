The context.

Bridge was a custom platform built for Utah Foster Care — a space for UFC employees and foster parents to communicate, collaborate, and build community around foster children. The mission was genuinely meaningful. The adoption problem was getting in the way of it.

Employees were logging in, getting confused, and going back to whatever they used before. The feedback kept pointing to the same thing: people didn't understand what different sections were for or how to move around the platform. And foster parents were about to be onboarded — the team didn't want to bring a new group of users into something existing users were already struggling with.

We had three weeks to fix it.

Starting with research.

My instinct before touching any designs was to figure out what actually makes a product tour work — because I'd seen enough bad onboarding to know it can make things worse, not better. The kind where there's a tooltip on every single element and you just click through all of it without reading anything.

I looked at a range of products and UI patterns, trying to understand what drove actual comprehension versus what felt like noise. That shaped my thinking pretty directly. The goal wasn't to explain everything Bridge could do — it was to get users oriented around enough that they felt confident continuing on their own.

The design challenge.

Bridge's navigation was fully customizable — section names and icons could be swapped per program. Which meant the tour couldn't rely on hardcoded labels. It had to pull in whatever names UFC had actually chosen, dynamically. That was a constraint I didn't fully appreciate until I started designing, and it narrowed what was technically feasible pretty quickly.

Deciding what to highlight.

I worked with our product owner and UFC's customer success manager to figure out what the tour actually needed to cover. We kept scope tight: main navigation, how to start a chat, key features within chat including video, and how to find support.

That last one I felt strongly about. If someone got stuck and didn't know where to go for help, they were going to disengage — and then we'd be back to the original problem. Making support findable wasn't an afterthought. It was part of the core job.

Working within real constraints.

With three weeks on the clock I didn't have the luxury of waiting until I had polished mockups to find out what was actually buildable. I brought sketches to the developer early — partly to share direction, mostly to pressure-test it. That conversation changed the design more than any review did. Some things I'd assumed were straightforward weren't, and catching that at the sketch stage was a lot cheaper than catching it later.

We iterated a few times before landing somewhere that worked within the technical constraints and the timeline. Then I moved into high-fidelity.

How it landed.

We shipped before the foster parent onboarding started. The tour gave both user groups — UFC employees who were already in the platform and foster parents coming in fresh — a structured starting point instead of just being dropped in and left to figure it out.

It's still live. That's the part I didn't fully expect. A lot of work that gets built under a tight deadline ends up getting replaced when there's more time to do it properly. This one held up.

3 weeks from brief to shipped
2 user groups onboarded — UFC employees and foster parents

1st professional project, real users, real stakes

What I took away.

Looking back, this is the project that taught me to bring things to developers early. Not when they're polished — early, when they're still rough enough to actually change. I'd been defaulting to cleaning things up before sharing them, which I think was partly just wanting to look like I knew what I was doing. The three-week timeline forced me out of that and I haven't really gone back.

The other thing that stays with me is who was actually using it. Foster care workers and families navigating a hard situation. That's not an abstract user — it's a specific person with a lot going on, trying to use a tool that's supposed to help them. Getting that right felt like it mattered in a way that was different from other projects.
